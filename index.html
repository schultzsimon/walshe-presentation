The New Discipline

Jennifer Walshe's concept of "The New Discipline" represents a framework for understanding interdisciplinary compositional practices that extend beyond traditional notation-centered approaches to music.

"The New Discipline is a term I've adopted to describe compositional work which falls between disciplines, drawing from experimental practices in composition, performance art, theatre and visual art." â€” Jennifer Walshe, 2016

Developed in the 2010s, this perspective acknowledges that contemporary performers are often required to vocalize, play instruments, move, and engage with different media and technologies simultaneously.

The New Discipline exists at the intersection of:

Composition

Performance Art

Visual Art

Digital Media

Key Principles

  • Embodiment

    The performer's physical presence is integral to the work.

  • Disciplinary Fluidity

    Moving beyond traditional boundaries of music composition.

  • Technological Integration

    Embracing digital tools and media as compositional elements.

  • Contextual Awareness

    Engaging with social, political, and cultural contexts directly.

Jennifer Walshe

Composer, performer, and multimedia artist

Born in Dublin, Ireland (1974)
Royal Scottish Academy, Northwestern University
DAAD Fellowship, Foundation for Contemporary Arts

Jennifer Walshe is an Irish composer, performer, and multimedia artist whose work seamlessly crosses boundaries between music, performance art, visual art, and technology. Her compositions often call for unconventional performance techniques and incorporate elements from diverse disciplines.

As both a theorist and practitioner, Walshe has been at the forefront of defining new approaches to interdisciplinary composition through her concept of "The New Discipline." She continues to push boundaries in her exploration of voice, technology, and embodiment in performance.

Notable Works

EVERYTHING IS IMPORTANT (2016)

A multimedia work for voice, string quartet and film that explores information overload in the digital age.

Historical Documents of the Irish Avant-Garde

A fictional revisionist history project creating an alternative musical past for Ireland.

THE TOTAL MOUNTAIN (2014)

A solo vocal performance work that incorporates extended techniques and physical theater.

AI: A Sound Artist (2022-present)

Explorations of artificial intelligence as a collaborative tool in sound art creation.

Ergomimesis: A Theoretical Framework

Thor Magnusson's concept of "ergomimesis" provides a valuable theoretical framework for understanding Walshe's approach to interdisciplinary art and new technologies. The term combines "ergo" (work) and "mimesis" (imitation), referring to how we model our interactions with technology based on previous tools and experiences.

"Ergomimesis is a form of imitation; not of the natural world, but rather of human activities, of gestures, movements and behaviors." â€” Thor Magnusson, "Sonic Writing" (2019)

In the context of Walshe's work, ergomimesis can be seen in how she interacts with and incorporates digital technologies while maintaining embodied performance practices. This approach allows for a fluid relationship between human physicality and technological mediation.

Ergomimesis in Practice

When artists like Walshe work with AI tools, they often approach them through ergomimetic frameworksâ€”applying familiar creative workflows and adapting them to new technological contexts. This creates a productive tension between embodied knowledge and emerging digital possibilities.

Key Aspects of Ergomimesis

  • Interface Design

    How technological interfaces reference familiar objects and actions

  • Physical Gestures

    The translation of embodied movements into digital interactions

  • Cultural Memory

    How previous tools shape our understanding of new technologies

  • Technological Evolution

    How new tools both reference and transform previous practices

Historical Examples of Flexible Mindsets

The flexible, interdisciplinary approach that characterizes Walshe's work has important historical precedents. Throughout the 20th century, numerous artists developed practices that crossed traditional boundaries and anticipated today's fluid relationships between different media and technologies.

John Cage (1912-1992)

Pioneered indeterminacy and multimedia composition, blurring boundaries between music, performance, and visual art. Works like "4'33"" (1952) challenged fundamental conceptions of music and silence.

Fluxus Movement (1960s)

An international collective of artists including Nam June Paik, Yoko Ono, and George Brecht who created "event scores" and performances that merged music, visual art, and everyday activities.

Laurie Anderson (b. 1947)

Developed a distinctive practice combining storytelling, electronic music, visual projection, and invented instruments. Her work "United States" (1983) exemplified this multimedia approach.

Connecting Past to Present

These historical examples demonstrate that the interdisciplinary approach championed by Walshe has deep roots in 20th-century avant-garde practices. However, contemporary practitioners like Walshe are extending these approaches into new technological realms, particularly through engagement with artificial intelligence and digital media.

Pauline Oliveros (1932-2016)

Developed "Deep Listening" practices that integrated technology, meditation, and embodied experience. Her approach to sound transcended conventional musical categories.

The Wooster Group (1975-present)

Theater collective that pioneered the integration of video, sound technology, and unconventional performance techniques, creating intermedial work that defies traditional categorization.

Meredith Monk (b. 1942)

Created "interdisciplinary performance works" combining extended vocal techniques, movement, and visual elements long before such approaches became more widespread.

Walshe's AI Projects

In recent years, Jennifer Walshe has turned her attention to artificial intelligence as both a subject and a tool in her creative practice. Her work explores the creative possibilities of AI while maintaining a critical perspective on its implications.

ULTRACHUNK (2018)

A collaboration with composer Memo Akten using machine learning algorithms trained on Walshe's vocal improvisations. The AI system learned to generate new material based on Walshe's distinctive vocal style.

This project raises questions about authorship, originality, and the relationship between human performers and their digital doubles.

AI: A SOUND ARTIST (2022-)

An ongoing project where Walshe explores using large language models and other AI tools to generate text, sound, and visual material for her compositions and performances.

The project examines how AI systems "understand" and represent artistic concepts, as well as the potential for meaningful collaboration between human and machine intelligence.

"I'm interested in AI not just as a tool, but as a collaborator that brings its own strange perspective and creative possibilities." â€” Jennifer Walshe, 2022

Key Themes in Walshe's AI Work

  • Human-Machine Collaboration

    Exploring creative partnerships between artists and AI systems

  • Digital Identity

    Questioning the nature of identity in relation to digital clones

  • Machine Learning as Composition

    Using AI training as a form of compositional process

  • Critical Perspectives

    Maintaining critical awareness of AI's social implications

My Voice Cloning Practice

My own artistic practice explores the creative possibilities of voice cloning technology and digital body doubles, building on the interdisciplinary approaches pioneered by artists like Jennifer Walshe.

Voice Cloning Project

Using deep learning techniques, I've developed a system that can analyze and replicate the distinctive qualities of my own voice. This "digital vocal twin" serves as both an instrument and a collaborator in my compositional process.

Technical Process

  1. Collection of vocal samples across different expressive modes
  2. Training of neural network models on this dataset
  3. Development of real-time control interfaces
  4. Integration with other media and performance elements
  5. Critical reflection and documentation of the process

Digital Body Doubles

Expanding beyond voice, I've also begun experimenting with visual representations and movement capture to create more complete digital avatars. These raise fascinating questions about identity, presence, and the boundaries between physical and virtual performance.

Results & Reflections

Working with my own digital clone has revealed unexpected creative possibilities, but also raised important ethical considerations. The uncanny experience of hearing "myself" perform material I never recorded has prompted deeper thinking about identity and authorship in the age of AI.

Like Walshe, I'm interested in maintaining a critical perspective while exploring these new technologies. My practice involves not just technical development but also philosophical inquiry into what these tools mean for our understanding of artistic expression.

Key Projects

Echoes of Self (2023)

A performance piece involving live interaction with my voice clone

Digital Chorus (2024)

Multiplying my voice into a virtual ensemble of different vocal personalities

Presence/Absence (2024)

Exploring performance scenarios where my physical and digital selves interact

Connections Between Approaches

The connections between Jennifer Walshe's "New Discipline" approach and my own practice with voice cloning reveal important insights about contemporary interdisciplinary art in the age of artificial intelligence.

Shared Elements

  • Crossing disciplinary boundaries
  • Critical engagement with technology
  • Focus on embodiment and presence
  • Questioning traditional notions of authorship
  • Process as important as product

Differences

  • Walshe's broader focus vs. my specific technological exploration
  • Different historical entry points into interdisciplinary practice
  • Variations in approach to performance documentation
  • Distinct relationships to institutional contexts

Both approaches exemplify what Thor Magnusson's concept of ergomimesis helps us understand: how artists adapt to new technologies while bringing embodied knowledge and critical perspectives to their engagement with these tools.

Broader Implications

The dialogue between Walshe's work and my own practice points to important questions facing contemporary artists working with new technologies:

1

How can we maintain critical perspectives while exploring new technological possibilities?

2

What happens to embodiment and presence when working with digital doubles?

3

How do we conceptualize authorship in collaborative work with AI systems?

4

What ethical considerations emerge when creating digital replicas of ourselves?

Conclusion

Jennifer Walshe's concept of "The New Discipline" provides a valuable framework for understanding contemporary interdisciplinary artistic practices that cross traditional boundaries. Her work, along with Thor Magnusson's theoretical concept of ergomimesis, helps us understand the complex relationships between human creativity and technological tools.

As we've seen through both Walshe's projects and my own practice with voice cloning and digital body doubles, artists today are engaging with new technologies in ways that both extend and transform established creative approaches. The historical examples we've examined show that this crossing of boundaries has deep roots, even as the specific technologies continue to evolve.

What emerges from this exploration is the importance of maintaining flexible mindsets in interdisciplinary art practiceâ€”approaches that can adapt to new technological possibilities while remaining grounded in critical awareness and embodied knowledge.

Future Directions

As AI and other emerging technologies continue to develop, artists working in the spirit of "The New Discipline" will play an essential role in exploring their creative potential, critiquing their limitations, and imagining new ways of being human in increasingly technological environments.

Key Takeaways

  • Interdisciplinary approaches have rich historical precedents

  • Physical embodiment remains crucial even in digital contexts

  • Critical engagement is as important as technical innovation

  • Ergomimesis helps us understand our relationships with technology

  • Flexible mindsets are essential for navigating emerging technological landscapes
